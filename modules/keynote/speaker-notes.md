## Per slide

1. **Cover.** People are settling. One line on who you are; the title does the framing.
2. **The pace.** The opener is a question, not a claim, so resist answering it here. Walk the timeline left to right: GPT-4 in early 2023, then releases pile up and accelerate into 2026, ending on Opus 4.8 and Fable 5 (which shipped a week before this keynote). The dots cascade in on entry; let them finish. The point is the cadence, not any single model: the ground moves every few weeks. One click: the surface keeps moving too, tool use, long context, reasoning modes, caching, pricing tiers. Leave the question hanging, slide 3 answers it. (Re-check the dots the week of the event. The optional spoken cost beat, no longer on the chart: GPT-4 $30 to GPT-5 $1.25 per million input tokens, roughly 96% cheaper.)
3. **Newer is not better, it is different.** The answer to slide 2, in two beats. First the catch: a newer model is not a clean upgrade, it does the same job another way, so dropping it into your pipeline brings side effects you have to find, and just keeping up is a job. Second click, the resolution: you stop betting on one model and build a system around them, robust to the churn and able to prove a swap still delivers. This is the twist and the so-what that used to be clicks 2 and 3 of the pace slide; it now stands on its own and seeds the whole talk.
4. **The online promise.** Open with the hype, not the gap: online it looks easy, buy the tool and jump to 10x. The three cards build that promised ladder (the +30% and 10x are the claimed numbers, from the CTO bootcamp keynote; say so out loud, it bridges the two events). Then the footer is the real point, revealed last: in practice it is easier said than done. Most teams buy the license, bank the +30%, and stall. The last step is a different way of working, not a faster autocomplete.
5. **Mindset, not tooling.** Frame it as the trap: we get stuck as the human in the loop. Read the room with warmth, every reason is legitimate. The middle items are the developer-honest ones: we liked writing code and got a review job instead, the human in the loop is tired, the click-monkey days are real. Land the last item as the actual stake: the role is becoming manager of agents, and pretending otherwise is what keeps teams stuck at +30%. (The old "none of it goes away by buying a license" footer is dropped for now; the last item carries the close.)
6. **Vibe coding.** The fast loop, spinning frantic. Where the easy-win noise comes from: instant demos, real dopamine, no compounding. Don't mock it; it is fine for prototypes, and that honesty buys you the next slide.
7. **Agentic engineering.** Same shape, different discipline: slower per iteration, faster per delivered feature, because verification is inside it. First taste of "system". The two loops echo on purpose.
8. **Bottleneck moved.** Pause here. The hinge of the talk.
9. **Speed stops hiding problems.** Your abstract, in your words. The four cards are discussion fuel for the pods later; don't resolve them now.
10. **The real shift.** Land the team message: the factory is shaped by how the team wants to collaborate. The third item is the shadow IT beat: non-engineers vibe code prototypes and expect production; the factory decides whether that arrives as intake or as a fight. Sets up Day 2 afternoon.
11. **Starting is simple.** Honest empathy, straight from the CTO bootcamp: the easy part really is easy (workshop, aha, hooked; most of this room is there). Click: and then, uncharted territory. No standard, no handbook, no reference org, no safe path. Don't rush "no safe path": it is why a bootcamp where you build with peers beats a blog post. Justifies the next two days.
12. **Maturity ladder.** Let people silently place their team while you walk L0 to L4. First click, the industry numbers: 90% use AI ~2h/day (DORA 2025), half of professionals daily, while 46% distrust output (Stack Overflow 2025). Usage up, trust DOWN: the plateau from slide 4 in different clothes. Above L1 the public numbers run out, which is why you build it here. Second click, the reframe: maturity asks where you are; your org asks where delivery will be in six months. Same ladder, a delivery question. Returns as kickoff question 4.
13. **Token economy.** Quick beat, 20 seconds. Test-time compute made token volume the new gospel, and the people selling the compute agree loudest. He is not wrong about capability; he is also not neutral. Pause after the quote, click the punchline. Bridge: our answer is fewer tokens that do more.
14. **AI that helps, instead of overhead.** The anti-hype slide: fewer tokens, quality designed in, correct the process not the output, the factory as a product the team owns. Hand-fixing a diff teaches nothing; fixing the rule or check removes the class of mistake. "Context discipline is cost discipline" is Björn's. Bridge into 15: so what does such a system look like? Someone already built one.
15. **Someone already built one.** Point at the photos: two of your trainers spent three months building a real AI factory that ships production software and still changes every week. Credibility and honesty at once: real, and a reference, not the answer. Warm and short, the next slides do the work. (Björn is on stage tomorrow for the hycle walkthrough; Bene co-founded h&w.)
16. **Phase 1, Mindset.** First of four. Two questions, not settings: are we optimizing for the demo or for next year (AI piles up debt faster), and where does AI cut corners (happy path strong, edge cases / observability / security weak). This phase is the lens for all four parts, not a single pod block, so the build tag says so. Light: read the two questions, do not lecture.
17. **Phase 2, Environment.** The foundations phase, maps to Pod block 1 this morning. Three questions: how much can the agent touch (sandboxed box), what does it load and at what cost (context discipline = cost discipline), what are the reusable parts (skills compose, sub-agents get fresh context and least privilege). Point forward: you set this up this morning.
18. **Phase 3, Process.** The biggest phase, maps to Pod block 2 this afternoon. Six questions, the pipeline itself: plan well, small increments, verify the plan, fast signal, determinism (encode don't instruct), trust-but-verify. Don't read all six slowly, sweep them: this is what "wire the pipeline" means, and it is this afternoon's work.
19. **Phase 4, Continuous improvement.** Maps to Pod block 3 tomorrow morning. Two questions: does every bug improve the system (turn each fix into a rule, check or skill), and is it still good after a model swap (treat the pipeline like a dependency, measure then compare). This is the seed of the whole Day 2 evals session.
20. **The whole factory.** The payoff: shows all thirteen at once after the four phases. No clicks, it just lands. Say the shape out loud: this many moving parts is a product, not a prompt. This is what Björn and Bene actually run.
21. **Same questions. Different answers.** The load-bearing reframe (the old no-blueprint beat). Their answers fit their use cases and this month's models; yours differ and keep moving as you mature (ladder callback) and as models change (slides 2-3 callback). We hand you the questions, not a copy. The relief: nobody expects you to leave with their factory, you leave able to build and re-build your own.
22. **Did this change make things better or worse?** Björn's actual evaluation question. Read it, then the catch: "better" has no single meaning. Walk the four constraints: cost (fewer tokens), time (faster per feature), quality (fewer escaped defects, lower change-failure), trust (confidence and evidence). Footer: you cannot max all four, so name your constraint first, then measure. That is tomorrow morning, the evals session: data, not vibes. (If asked how to measure: h&w's KPIs.)
23. **Four phases become four parts.** The bridge, and the answer to "how does Björn's structure map to our two days." His mindset + environment become Part 1 (foundations, this morning); his process becomes Part 2 (pipeline, this afternoon); his continuous improvement becomes Part 3 (run it as a product, tomorrow morning); Part 4 (take it to the org, tomorrow afternoon) is the bootcamp's addition beyond his engineering pipeline. Colors match the phase slides on purpose. This slide returns at the start of every block.
24. **Impact range.** Numbers attributed to the CTO bootcamp out loud. Then the caveat on its own click: nobody publishes how these are measured, one number called quality means different things everywhere. Not a footnote, it is tomorrow morning: you define the metric for your factory. (Callback to slide 22.)
25. **Take home.** Energizing close of the talk proper: running factory, a way to measure it (Day 2 seed: data, not vibes), 90-day rollout. "You are early" releases the FOMO pressure.
26. **Agenda.** Fast walk-through, 30 seconds. The "now" chip does the work.
27. **Trainers.** Point at the people in the room rather than reading the cards. (Björn and Bene already met the room on slide 15.)
28. **House rules.** Brief and friendly: the rule protects the war stories, especially the customer reports at 16:30.
29. **Pod kickoff.** Switch from talk to instructions. Five questions, everyone answers, honesty beats polish. Question 4 (what is being pushed in your org) is the maturity-ladder reframe from slide 12: where is delivery expected to be in six months. The footer is the payoff: the table's shared problems become the working groups for the rest of the bootcamp. Facilitators keep time per FACILITATION.md.
30. **Output + handover.** Each pod pins one headline + one open question to the wall; they stay up for both days and feed the show & tell tomorrow. Send people to their pods, see them at 11:00.
